Biometric Identification Through Auditory EEG Signatures

ICLR Submitted: December 31, 2025

Summary

The paper proposes a two-stage EEG-based biometric authentication framework that targets robustness and open-set operation. Stage I applies a WaveNet-based denoiser trained with a scale-invariant signal-to-noise ratio (SI-SNR) objective to suppress environmental/physiological noise. Stage II employs a ResNet backbone trained with deep metric learning (e.g., ArcFace, Multi-Similarity) to produce discriminative embeddings for identity retrieval and open-set rejection via distance thresholds. Experiments on a 20-subject auditory EEG dataset report P@1 near 1.0 under some noise conditions and roughly 98% “open-set accuracy,” and ablations suggest improvements from the denoising stage.

Strengths

  • Technical novelty and innovation
    • The paper combines time-domain denoising with metric-learning-based embeddings for open-set EEG authentication, an intuitively sound and practically motivated system-level design.
    • Use of angular-margin losses (ArcFace) and multi-similarity loss for EEG biometrics is aligned with best practices in face recognition and recent EEG works.
  • Experimental rigor and validation
    • Includes ablations showing the denoiser’s contribution under different synthetic noise types (Gaussian, power-line, EMG).
    • Evaluates multiple losses and ResNet backbones and reports consistent trends (Multi-Similarity and ArcFace performing best).
  • Clarity of presentation
    • The high-level pipeline and open-set decision rule (prototype + threshold) are described clearly enough to grasp the intended operation.
    • Motivation for decoupling denoising from embedding learning is articulated.
  • Significance of contributions
    • Open-set EEG authentication and robustness to noise remain important practical challenges, and the paper’s framing is relevant to deployable biometric systems.
    • The focus on auditory-evoked EEG is timely given reported benefits of auditory stimulation for EEG biometrics.

Weaknesses

  • Technical limitations or concerns
    • The subject-independent protocol and reported retrieval metrics appear contradictory: if test subjects are unseen during training, rank-1 identification among enrolled identities is ill-defined. The paper needs to clearly separate closed-set identification (known subjects) from open-set verification (unseen subjects) and align metrics accordingly.
    • The open-set evaluation is under-specified: TAR is reported without corresponding FAR or ROC/DET/EER; “98% open-set accuracy” is ambiguous and not a standard biometric measure, making the claim difficult to assess.
    • Denoising with WaveNet on multichannel EEG lacks architectural detail (channel handling, receptive field, dilation schedule, causal vs non-causal), training regimen (noise simulation protocol, SNR ranges, loss weighting), and justification of SI-SNR for EEG beyond time-domain speech separation.
  • Experimental gaps or methodological issues
    • No comparison to strong, recent EEG denoisers (e.g., RetNet-based EEGDiR) or simpler baselines (bandpass/notch, ICA, TCN/UNet) to contextualize the denoiser’s contribution.
    • The dataset is small (N=20), and results lack variance estimates or statistical tests; robustness claims are weak given known scale effects in EEG biometrics (e.g., PEERS shows significant performance degradation at scale and over time).
    • Missing standard biometric analysis: EER vs time/session, ROC/DET curves at varying thresholds, FAR-constrained TAR (e.g., TAR at FAR = 1%, 0.1%), and calibration/threshold transfer across sessions/devices.
    • The “joint optimization” claim is not realized; stages appear trained separately with no end-to-end or joint objective.
  • Clarity or presentation issues
    • The metrics in Tables I–III are inconsistently defined (Accuracy%, P@1, TAR%) and not mapped to closed- vs open-set protocols; it’s unclear what “Accuracy (%)” in Table I measures.
    • Details about segmentation length, channel count, preprocessing, sampling rates, and data splits across sessions/conditions/headphones are missing.
  • Missing related work or comparisons
    • Recent, relevant works are not adequately discussed or compared against (e.g., large-scale PEERS benchmarking using supervised contrastive metric learning; RetNet-based EEG denoising; open-set EEG authentication literature and distance calibration techniques such as EVT/OpenMax).

Detailed Comments

  • Technical soundness evaluation
    • The overall two-stage design is conceptually sound and aligns with evidence from speech/ASR that front-end enhancement can help downstream recognition. Using angular-margin metric learning is appropriate for identity embeddings. However, technical soundness is undermined by missing implementation specifics (especially for WaveNet with multichannel EEG) and an unclear training/evaluation protocol. The use of SI-SNR for EEG is plausible but should be justified against EEG-specific fidelity measures and paired with denoising baselines to ensure the denoiser does not remove identity-bearing components.
    • Open-set modeling via Euclidean distance to class centroids with a fixed threshold is a reasonable starting point, but for deployable systems, calibration methods (e.g., EVT-based thresholding) and reporting across a range of operational points are expected.
  • Experimental evaluation assessment
    • The current evaluation is insufficient for ICLR standards. Key omissions include: (1) standard verification metrics (EER, ROC/DET, AUROC), (2) FAR-vs-TAR trade-off curves, (3) cross-session generalization analysis (train on session A, test on later sessions) and how thresholds transfer, (4) consideration of temporal degradation, (5) variance and statistical significance, and (6) comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., ResNet1D + SupCon, as indicated by PEERS).
    • The “subject-independent” statement conflicts with retrieval evaluation. The authors need two parallel protocols: (a) closed-set identification within the enrolled set, (b) open-set verification with known imposters and unknown subjects, reporting EER/TAR@FAR. As written, the reported “near-perfect P@1” and “98% open-set accuracy” are not interpretable.
    • Noise experiments appear to use synthetic contamination; the paper should describe noise generation, SNR ranges, and whether denoiser training/testing uses matched or mismatched noise types. Real-session artifacts and hardware variation should also be evaluated.
  • Comparison with related work (using the summaries provided)
    • The paper should position results against large-scale benchmarking (PEERS), where deep metric learning achieved EER ≈10% on 100 test subjects with significant temporal/device effects. Claims of deployability and near-perfect performance on a 20-subject dataset need to be tempered and contextualized.
    • For denoising, compare with EEGDiR (RetNet-based) and other modern EEG denoisers; include RRMSE/CC or EEG-centric fidelity metrics in addition to SI-SNR, and most importantly, show downstream gains on authentication to validate the premise.
    • Open-set EEG biometrics has relevant practices in threshold calibration and OSR metrics (e.g., OSCR, AUROC for unknown detection) that should be adopted. The paper’s current approach lacks such comparisons or references.
    • Related studies on auditory stimulation show benefits but also highlight small cohorts and missing long-term tests; this paper cites such datasets but does not exploit the multi-session structure to analyze permanence or cross-condition transfer (in-ear vs bone conduction).
  • Discussion of broader impact and significance
    • EEG biometrics implicates privacy and medical sensitivity (health inference, cognitive state). The paper briefly mentions ethics but should discuss template protection, revocability, on-device processing, and potential leakage from denoising or embeddings. Claims of “deployable solution” are premature without evaluations at scale, long-term stability, and security analyses (spoof resistance, adversarial robustness).
    • Still, the framing—separating enhancement from embedding learning for open-set authentication—is a useful template for future EEG biometric systems, especially if coupled with rigorous calibration and large-scale validation.

Questions for Authors

  1. Please clarify the exact training/testing splits: Which subjects are used for training the embedding model, which for enrollment, and which for testing? How are closed-set identification and open-set verification separated? If the test is subject-independent, how is P@1 (retrieval among enrolled identities) computed?
  2. How is multichannel EEG handled in the WaveNet denoiser (channel-wise, multi-input WaveNet, early/late fusion)? Provide architectural details (layers, dilation schedule, receptive field, causal/non-causal) and training hyperparameters.
  3. What preprocessing is applied (referencing, notch/bandpass filters, normalization), segmentation/window lengths, overlap, and channel selection? How many segments per subject/session are used?
  4. Please report standard verification metrics: ROC/DET curves, EER, and TAR at fixed FAR (1%, 0.1%). For open-set unknown detection, report AUROC/OSCR and provide the distribution of known/unknown queries.
  5. How are thresholds estimated and transferred across sessions and subjects? Do you evaluate calibration drift across sessions/headphone types? Any cross-condition generalization results (in-ear vs bone conduction)?
  6. What are the noise simulation protocols (SNR levels, mixing procedure)? Are denoiser training and testing matched or mismatched in noise types/SNRs? Can you compare WaveNet to simpler denoisers (bandpass/ICA/Notch) and to modern neural denoisers (e.g., EEGDiR) in both SI-SNR and downstream authentication?
  7. The paper states the importance of “jointly optimizing noise mitigation and embedding learning,” yet the method is staged. Did you attempt end-to-end fine-tuning or multi-task training? If not, please adjust the claim or provide experiments.
  8. Please compare against supervised contrastive learning (SupCon) with a ResNet1D encoder and Euclidean distance, as this is a strong baseline in large-scale EEG biometrics.
  9. Can you provide variance estimates (e.g., across folds/sessions) and significance testing for the reported improvements?
  10. Will you release code, training scripts, and exact splits to enable reproducibility?

Overall Assessment

Estimated Score: 4.4/10 (Calibrated to ICLR scale)

The paper addresses an important and practical problem—robust, open-set EEG authentication—and proposes a sensible two-stage pipeline integrating denoising and metric-learning embeddings. The high-level idea is reasonable and potentially useful. However, the current version falls short of ICLR standards in experimental rigor and clarity. Key protocol ambiguities (especially around subject independence vs retrieval), missing standard biometric metrics (EER/ROC/DET, TAR@FAR), lack of strong denoising baselines and modern metric-learning comparisons, and limited scale (20 subjects) make the reported near-perfect performance and deployability claims unconvincing. The work would be strengthened substantially by a clear evaluation protocol separating closed- and open-set settings, comprehensive verification metrics, comparisons to strong recent baselines (e.g., ResNet1D+SupCon, EEGDiR denoising), architectural details and reproducible splits, and analyses of cross-session/condition generalization with proper calibration. As it stands, I recommend rejection, with encouragement to revise along the above lines.
